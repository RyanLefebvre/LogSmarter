What Is TDEE And Why Is It Important?
Total daily energy expenditure (TDEE) is the energy, in calories, used by someone during a 24-hour period. TDEE can be split into three main components: Basal metabolic rate (BMR) The thermic effect of food (TEF) and Non-exercise adaptive thermogenesis (NEAT).
BMR is the largest fraction of TDEE comprising up to 75% and is defined as energy cost during absolute rest like when you’re sleeping.
Non-exercise adaptive thermogenesis (NEAT) are the calories you burn through fidgeting, staying upright, and all other physical activities except purposeful exercise. NEAT contributes ~ 10-15% to your TDEE.
The thermic effect of food (TEF) is the increase in energy expenditure associated with digestion, absorption and storage of food, and accounts for approximately 10% of the total daily energy expenditure; many believe there exist facultative as well as fixed components, yet this can be changed with the macronutrient composition of a meal.
Another component of TDEE not mentioned is physical activity. This is because physical activity varies between people. See our resource on how to choose your activity level for more information. Generally, if you eat less than your TDEE you will lose weight and if you eat more than your TDEE you will gain weight. Therefore, if you have health, physique, or sports performance-related goals, knowing your TDEE is essential for optimizing your body composition, performance, and nutrition.
